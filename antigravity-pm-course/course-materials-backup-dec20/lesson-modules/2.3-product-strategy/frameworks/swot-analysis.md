# SWOT Analysis Framework

## Overview

SWOT Analysis is a classic strategic planning framework used to evaluate **S**trengths, **W**eaknesses, **O**pportunities, and **T**hreats. While it's been around since the 1960s, it remains valuable for understanding your strategic position before making decisions.

**Origin:** Developed at Stanford Research Institute in the 1960s-70s

**Best Used For:**
- Initial strategic assessment
- Competitive landscape analysis
- Understanding internal capabilities
- Identifying external market forces
- Pre-planning environmental scan

**Limitations:**
- Doesn't tell you WHAT to do (just identifies factors)
- Can become a laundry list without prioritization
- Static snapshot (doesn't account for dynamics)
- Best combined with frameworks like Rumelt's Kernel or DHM

---

## The Four Quadrants

### INTERNAL FACTORS (What you control)

#### STRENGTHS
**What you're good at. Your advantages.**

**Questions to ask:**
- What do we do better than competitors?
- What unique resources or capabilities do we have?
- What do customers say we excel at?
- What advantages do we have? (team, tech, data, brand, etc.)
- What's working well right now?

**Examples (TaskFlow AI Strategy):**
- ✅ Existing voice chat infrastructure
- ✅ Small team focus (5-20 person teams) - underserved segment
- ✅ Strong activation & onboarding expertise
- ✅ Product simplicity (easier to use than Notion/Asana)
- ✅ Nimble (can ship faster than enterprises)

---

#### WEAKNESSES
**What you're not good at. Your disadvantages.**

**Questions to ask:**
- What do competitors do better than us?
- What resources or capabilities are we missing?
- What do customers complain about?
- What internal limitations do we face?
- What's not working right now?

**Examples (TaskFlow AI Strategy):**
- ❌ Limited engineering resources (only 2 engineers)
- ❌ Small budget ($50k for H1 2026)
- ❌ No ML/AI specialists on team
- ❌ Low brand recognition vs Notion/Linear/Asana
- ❌ No existing AI features (starting from zero)
- ❌ Smaller customer base = less training data

---

### EXTERNAL FACTORS (What you don't control)

#### OPPORTUNITIES
**External conditions you could exploit.**

**Questions to ask:**
- What market trends favor us?
- What are competitors NOT doing?
- What customer needs are unmet?
- What new technologies could we leverage?
- What partnerships could we pursue?

**Examples (TaskFlow AI Strategy):**
- ✅ SMBs are underserved by current AI tools
- ✅ Competitors focused on enterprise = open niche
- ✅ OpenAI/Anthropic APIs make AI accessible (don't need ML team)
- ✅ Voice interfaces becoming mainstream (users ready)
- ✅ Remote work trend = more small distributed teams
- ✅ AI hype creates openness to try new features

---

#### THREATS
**External risks to your success.**

**Questions to ask:**
- What are competitors doing that could hurt us?
- What market trends work against us?
- What technological changes could disrupt us?
- What customer behavior changes could hurt us?
- What economic/regulatory risks exist?

**Examples (TaskFlow AI Strategy):**
- ❌ Notion/Linear/Asana have 10x more resources
- ❌ They could easily build SMB-focused AI if they wanted
- ❌ AI features are becoming commodity/expected
- ❌ Users might consolidate tools (fewer SaaS subscriptions)
- ❌ OpenAI API costs could increase
- ❌ Enterprise competitors could move downmarket

## How to Conduct SWOT Analysis

### Step 1: Brainstorm Each Quadrant
- Set 10-minute timer for each quadrant
- List everything (don't filter yet)
- Involve team (diverse perspectives)
- Use data where possible, not just opinions

### Step 2: Validate with Evidence
- For each item, ask: "Is this actually true?"
- Strengths: Do customers/data confirm this?
- Weaknesses: Is this perception or reality?
- Opportunities: Is this real or wishful thinking?
- Threats: How likely is this really?

### Step 3: Prioritize
- Rank items by importance/impact
- Focus on top 3-5 per quadrant
- Use 2x2 matrix: Impact (high/low) x Certainty (high/low)

### Step 4: Turn Insights into Strategy
- **Strengths + Opportunities:** Where should you double down?
- **Strengths + Threats:** How can you defend?
- **Weaknesses + Opportunities:** What capabilities do you need to build?
- **Weaknesses + Threats:** What risks must you mitigate?

## SWOT to Strategy Mapping

### SO Strategy (Strengths + Opportunities)
**"Pursue" - Use your strengths to exploit opportunities**

**Example (TaskFlow):**
- **Strength:** Voice chat infrastructure
- **Opportunity:** Voice interfaces becoming mainstream
- **Strategy:** Build AI voice-to-task creation (leverage strength to capture opportunity)

---

### ST Strategy (Strengths + Threats)
**"Defend" - Use your strengths to mitigate threats**

**Example (TaskFlow):**
- **Strength:** Product simplicity, fast shipping
- **Threat:** Enterprise competitors could move downmarket
- **Strategy:** Ship SMB-specific AI features FAST before they notice the segment

---

### WO Strategy (Weaknesses + Opportunities)
**"Build" - Overcome weaknesses to capture opportunities**

**Example (TaskFlow):**
- **Weakness:** No ML/AI specialists
- **Opportunity:** OpenAI/Anthropic APIs available
- **Strategy:** Partner with API providers instead of building ML in-house

---

### WT Strategy (Weaknesses + Threats)
**"Mitigate" - Minimize weaknesses and avoid threats**

**Example (TaskFlow):**
- **Weakness:** Limited budget ($50k)
- **Threat:** AI features becoming commodity
- **Strategy:** Focus on ONE differentiated AI capability vs spreading budget thin on commodities

## Common Mistakes with SWOT

### Mistake 1: Lists Without Prioritization
- Creating 20-item lists for each quadrant
- **Fix:** Force rank and pick top 3-5 per quadrant

### Mistake 2: Internal vs External Confusion
- Listing "small budget" as a Threat (it's a Weakness)
- Listing "great team" as an Opportunity (it's a Strength)
- **Fix:** Strengths/Weaknesses = internal, Opportunities/Threats = external

### Mistake 3: Vague Statements
- "Good product" (not specific)
- "Market changes" (what changes?)
- **Fix:** Be specific and evidence-based

### Mistake 4: SWOT as Final Output
- Doing SWOT and stopping there
- **Fix:** SWOT is INPUT to strategy, not the strategy itself

### Mistake 5: One-Time Exercise
- Doing SWOT once and never updating
- **Fix:** Revisit quarterly as market/capabilities change

## SWOT + Other Frameworks

### SWOT → Rumelt's Kernel
1. **SWOT** identifies factors
2. **Diagnosis** (Rumelt) synthesizes SWOT into critical challenge
3. **Guiding Policy** addresses the challenge
4. **Coherent Actions** leverage strengths, mitigate weaknesses

**Example Flow:**
- SWOT shows: Strength (voice chat), Opportunity (SMB underserved), Threat (enterprise competitors)
- Diagnosis: "We can win SMB niche before enterprises notice"
- Guiding Policy: "Focus AI on SMB-specific voice features"
- Coherent Actions: Build voice-to-task, subsidize pricing, ship fast

---

### SWOT → DHM Framework
1. **SWOT** identifies what you're good at (Strengths)
2. **DHM** evaluates if those strengths can become moats

**Example:**
- SWOT Strength: "Existing voice infrastructure"
- DHM Hard-to-Copy: "Competitors would need 12-18 months to build voice from scratch"
- Result: Voice-based AI is defensible differentiation

## Real-World Example: Slack (circa 2014)

### STRENGTHS
- ✅ Real-time messaging that actually works
- ✅ Integrations with other tools (100+ at launch)
- ✅ Delightful UX (emoji reactions, search, threads)
- ✅ Viral freemium model (organic growth)
- ✅ Technical team from gaming (high-performance infrastructure)

### WEAKNESSES
- ❌ Small company vs Microsoft/Google
- ❌ Limited enterprise features (no SSO, compliance)
- ❌ Not yet profitable
- ❌ Dependent on third-party integrations
- ❌ No video/voice (just chat)

### OPPORTUNITIES
- ✅ Remote work trend growing
- ✅ Email overload creating demand for alternatives
- ✅ Developers tired of HipChat/IRC
- ✅ Enterprise messaging market = huge TAM
- ✅ Mobile-first work culture emerging

### THREATS
- ❌ Microsoft could bundle Teams with Office
- ❌ Facebook launching Workplace
- ❌ HipChat (Atlassian) has enterprise relationships
- ❌ Security concerns for enterprise adoption
- ❌ Switching costs from email are high

### STRATEGIC CHOICES (Based on SWOT)

**SO Strategy (Strengths + Opportunities):**
- Double down on developer-first positioning (strength in UX + opportunity in dev market)
- Expand integrations (leverage strength + capture enterprise opportunity)

**ST Strategy (Strengths + Threats):**
- Build enterprise features FAST (use freemium strength to defend against Microsoft)
- Focus on teams, not individuals (differentiate from email, defend against Workplace)

**WO Strategy (Weaknesses + Opportunities):**
- Build enterprise security/compliance (overcome weakness to capture enterprise opportunity)
- Partner for video/voice (overcome weakness without distracting from core)

**WT Strategy (Weaknesses + Threats):**
- Move upmarket quickly (mitigate small company weakness before Microsoft threat materializes)

**Result:** Slack grew to $400M ARR in 2 years by executing these strategies.

## When to Use SWOT

### SWOT is Great For:
✅ **Initial strategic assessment** - Understanding the landscape before planning
✅ **Quarterly strategy reviews** - What's changed in our position?
✅ **Competitive analysis** - What are we good/bad at vs competitors?
✅ **New market entry** - Should we pursue this opportunity?
✅ **Team alignment** - Getting shared understanding of situation
✅ **Pre-planning** - Before using Rumelt or DHM frameworks

### SWOT is NOT Great For:
❌ **Final strategy output** - It's analysis, not strategy
❌ **Feature prioritization** - Use DHM or RICE instead
❌ **Detailed competitive positioning** - Use Porter's Five Forces
❌ **Dynamic market modeling** - SWOT is a snapshot, not predictive

## SWOT Workshop Format (60 minutes)

**Materials:**
- Whiteboard or Miro board
- Sticky notes (4 colors)
- Team (5-8 people ideal)

**Agenda:**

**0-5min:** Frame the question
- What decision are we making?
- What timeframe? (H1 2026? 3-year vision?)

**5-25min:** Brainstorm (5min per quadrant)
- Strengths: What are we good at?
- Weaknesses: What are we not good at?
- Opportunities: What external factors favor us?
- Threats: What external risks do we face?
- Everyone writes ideas on sticky notes silently

**25-40min:** Cluster and discuss
- Group similar items
- Read out loud
- Clarify and debate
- Remove duplicates

**40-50min:** Prioritize
- Vote on top 3 per quadrant
- Discuss disagreements
- Focus on evidence

**50-60min:** Strategic implications
- What does this tell us?
- What should we do differently?
- What's the next step?

**Output:** Prioritized SWOT → Input for strategy development

---

## SWOT Analysis Checklist

Before finalizing SWOT, check:

✅ **Evidence-based:** Each item supported by data or feedback
✅ **Specific:** No vague statements like "good product"
✅ **Balanced:** Not just strengths and opportunities (be honest about weaknesses/threats)
✅ **Prioritized:** Top 3-5 per quadrant identified
✅ **Internal vs External:** Strengths/Weaknesses = internal, Opportunities/Threats = external
✅ **Actionable:** Can translate into strategy (not just observations)
✅ **Current:** Reflects today's reality, not outdated info
✅ **Relevant:** Focused on the specific strategic question at hand

## Further Reading

**Classic Resources:**
- *Competitive Strategy* by Michael Porter (complementary framework)
- *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy* by Richard Rumelt (SWOT → Strategy)
- Harvard Business Review: "SWOT Analysis" articles

**Modern Applications:**
- Combine SWOT with Jobs-to-be-Done for customer insight
- Use SWOT before OKR planning
- Apply SWOT to feature launches, not just company strategy

**Related Frameworks:**
- **Porter's Five Forces:** Industry structure analysis
- **PESTLE:** Macro-environmental factors (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental)
- **Value Chain Analysis:** Where you create value internally

---

**Remember:** SWOT is a starting point for strategic thinking, not the end point. Use it to understand your position, then apply frameworks like Rumelt's Kernel or DHM to decide what to do about it.
